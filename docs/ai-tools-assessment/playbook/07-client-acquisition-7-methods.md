# Seven Ways to Get Clients (no capital, no audience)

Every one of these works without money or a following. The catch is that almost nobody does them, and the few who do quit after three days. Hang in for seven days and you are in the top fraction of a percent.

## 1. Host a local "AI for business" meetup

Partner with a co-working space: "I'll bring 30 local owners into your space for exposure. Can I use your conference room for an hour, free?" You are the host, so you are automatically the expert. Show up, network 10 to 15 minutes, give a 20-minute talk on how to use Claude (simple enough to follow, advanced enough to wow), collect contact info at the door, and follow up within 24 hours offering the assessment.

This is the SEO of client-getting. Your clients come from the third, fourth, fifth, sixth event, not day one. Run it monthly and become the local AI event brand.

## 2. Door knocking

The fastest path. Walk into local service businesses, ask for the owner or manager, offer a free mini-audit. It is also the single best way to learn sales. Results are near-immediate: one person who heard this idea knocked 30 businesses, set 5 meetings, and closed 2 clients. Need a client this week? Knock a door.

## 3. LinkedIn DMs

Target local owners in your city. Probe, do not pitch in message one. Ask how they are using AI today and offer free feedback.

> "Hey Joe, my name's Corey. I probably live down the street from you. Curious how you're using AI in your business. Would love to give you some free feedback on ways you could implement it."

Voice messages and Looms work well here. "I probably live down the street from you" is a strong local hook.

## 4. Free mini-audits for your network

You already know business owners: gym, church, other parents at your kids' events. A quick text: "I'm getting into the AI services world. I'd love to spend 15 minutes with you, free, and show you one way AI could help your business. Open to it?" The mini-audit is the lead magnet; the full assessment is the upsell.

Frame it worst-case to make it irresistible: "Worst case, you learn one or two AI tools you'd never heard of. Best case, you get a partner who can build this stuff for you." When the worst case is still a win, they have nothing to lose.

## 5. Agency partners

Insurance agents, accountants, marketing agencies, coaches, consultants: their clients are your ICP and are already asking them about AI. Offer a referral fee for anyone they send who becomes a client. The key is follow-up: check in every two to three weeks ("any clients ask about AI lately? Happy to be a resource"). Level it up with a co-branded workshop, for example "AI to optimize your finances, brought to you by [you] and [the accounting firm]."

**Best combo:** pair method 5 with method 1. Corey's first meetup was hosted with a plugged-in local realtor who invited her clients and other realtors. Got a client out of it.

## 6. AI office hours

Approach a co-working space: "Let me come in one hour a week and be a free AI resource for your tenants. Any question, they walk up and ask." Win-win-win: the space adds value, tenants get free consulting, and you plug straight into a room full of ICP. Do a good job and they naturally want to hire you.

> A guy named Dennis reached out to ~10 spaces, got 2 to agree. Before the first session he door-knocked local businesses for **$175 in free gift cards** to use as a draw, got 11 people to show, and left with 2 follow-up calls. Office hours plus door knocking plus initiative.

## 7. Post your wins

Build in public. Document as you go: short videos, X posts, images. A "win" does not need to be "I saved someone 40 hours." It can be "someone texted me a ChatGPT question and I gave a great answer, here it is." Early on you have little to show, so manufacture momentum: commit to one valuable prompt a day for 90 days as carousels, stick to it, and layer in real client results as they arrive.

## The multiplier: niche down

Pick a geography or a vertical and own it. "The AI guy in San Antonio" (geographic, which is exactly what aiconsultingsa.com plays for) or "the AI guy for financial services" (vertical, if you have the background). Niching lets you charge more and shields you from price pressure, because you are the obvious person for that specific client. The pie is effectively infinite; the win is being the one they think of first.
